Operators are positioned between their operands, representing the conventional method of expressing mathematical operations. An expression like A * ( B + C ) / D typically implies a specific sequence of actions: "First, add B and C together, then multiply the result by A, and finally, divide by D to obtain the ultimate result." In infix notation, additional details are required to clarify the order of operator evaluation. This involves inherent language rules governing operator precedence and associativity, as well as the use of brackets ( ) to enable users to override these default rules. For instance, standard rules of associativity dictate a left-to-right order of operations, implying that the multiplication by A takes precedence over the division by D. Similarly, established rules of precedence prioritize multiplication and division before addition and subtraction.
Department of Computer Science, University of Manchester What defines an infix expression?

Operands precede operators in written expressions. The given infix expression, A B C + * D /, is synonymous with the stated order of A, B, C, addition, multiplication, and division. The sequence of operator evaluation follows a consistent left-to-right pattern, and the arrangement cannot be altered using brackets. In the provided example, as "+" precedes "*", addition is performed before multiplication. Operators directly affect the values immediately to their left; for instance, the "+" involves the values "B" and "C." To emphasize this, unnecessary brackets can be added, such as ( (A (B C +) *) D /), clarifying that multiplication employs the preceding values "A" and the sum of "B" and "C." Similarly, division utilizes the product of multiplication and the value "D." This notation is also referred to as "Reverse Polish Notation."
